A Photo Essay · 1995 — 2023

On Film

Twenty-eight years measured not in days, but in rolls of Portra — a life spent chasing the light before it goes.

Words & Photographs by Tom Milton
Scroll
Chapter One

The Weight of Light

Tuscany · The Cascades · Olympic National Park

There is a moment, perhaps an hour before the sun gives up the day, when the world stops pretending. The fields exhale. The shadows grow honest and long. I have spent the better part of my life waiting for that hour, a camera warm against my palm, a single roll of film holding thirty-six chances to keep what cannot be kept.

I came to film late, and stayed on purpose. Everyone I knew had gone digital by then — instant, endless, forgiving. But there is a particular kind of attention that only thirty-six frames will teach you. You learn to wait. You learn that the photograph is mostly made before the shutter ever opens, in the quiet arithmetic of light and patience.

Portra was always my film. It renders skin like memory does — softened, warmed, more forgiving than the truth. Greens fall back politely; the oranges and ambers step forward. It is not how the eye sees. It is how the heart remembers having seen.

That first autumn I drove the spine of Tuscany with no destination, only a feeling that the country had something to tell me if I slowed down enough to listen. I shot the wheat at the exact minute the sun touched the far ridge, and I knew — the way you sometimes simply know — that the frame was already good before the film was ever developed.

Mountains taught me humility; forests taught me silence. In the Cascades I learned that fog is not an obstacle to a photograph but the photograph itself — the way it erases the middle distance and leaves only the near and the suggested-far, a watercolour the camera can almost hold.

You do not photograph the light. You photograph what the light is willing to leave behind.
Field notebook, Olympic Peninsula, 1996
Chapter Two

The Long Way Home

The roads between places · 1999 — 2008

Most of my best photographs were taken on the way to somewhere else. The detour is the discipline. I learned to give myself the extra hour, the wrong turn, the gravel road that bends out of sight into fog — because that is where the country keeps its quiet things.

An hour before sunrise

The Dolomites do not wake gently. They loom out of the dark all at once, grey then blue then briefly, impossibly, pink. I shot this with the engine still ticking as it cooled, the headlights catching the first of the mist, the tungsten warmth of Cinestill turning the fog the colour of a half-remembered dream.

The river finds the lowest ground

Water always knows the way home, even when I don't. In Scotland I followed a river for a full day with no map, certain only that downstream meant somewhere. Velvia made the green almost violent — a saturated, jewelled green that no screen has ever quite reproduced.

And the coast, where the road runs out

Eventually every road I loved ended at water. Big Sur was the edge of the map and the edge of something in me too — the cliffs falling into the Pacific, the air thick with salt and the particular gold of a Portra afternoon. You stand there and understand why people once believed the world had an edge you could fall off.

There were years I drove more than I slept. Iceland in the rain, the Black Forest at the blue end of dusk, Vermont when the maples turned and the whole state seemed to be burning slowly and beautifully down. I kept a film log in a leather notebook, one line per frame, and reading it now is reading a map of my own restlessness.

Chapter Three

Still Water, Still Mornings

Banff · Hallstatt · the Lake District

A lake at dawn is the closest thing I know to a held breath. Everything doubled, everything paused. I have stood waist-deep in cold Austrian water at five in the morning to keep a tripod steady, shivering, grinning like a fool, because the mountains were repeating themselves perfectly in the glass of the lake and I had four frames left.

Ektar is a colder, sharper film than my beloved Portra — clinical, almost — and for water it is perfect. It keeps the blues honest and the whites clean. I save it for the still mornings, the ones that ask to be remembered exactly as they were rather than as I felt them.

The cabin at Banff I returned to three years running. Each time the light was different and the lake was the same, and I came to understand that this is the whole secret of landscape work: you are never photographing the place. You are photographing a single morning that will never happen again, in a place that will outlast you by ten thousand of them.

Once a year or so I would load a roll of black and white and shoot a forest until the colour drained out of how I was seeing. Norway in the snow, the pines barely there, the grain of HP5 doing what no Kodak ever could — turning silence into something you can almost hold in your two hands.

Film does not capture a moment. It agrees to remember one, on your behalf, long after you have forgotten how it felt.
Tom Milton
Chapter Four

The Faces We Keep

Lisbon · Havana · Paris · everywhere there were people

For years I told myself I was a landscape photographer because landscapes do not ask anything of you. They do not flinch or perform or look away. Then somewhere in my forties the work turned, the way a river turns, and I found I could not stop photographing faces — the small weather that moves across them, the light that gets caught in the corner of a smile.

A portrait on film is a negotiation. There is no chimping, no instant reassurance on a screen. You ask a stranger to trust you for a thirtieth of a second, and you trust the film to be there when the trust runs out. When it works, it is the most intimate thing a photograph can do.

This is where Portra finally came home. Built for skin, it renders a face the way affection does — warm, soft, generous with the highlights, kind to every imperfection it finds. I shot the woman by the window in Lisbon on a grey afternoon, the only light a tall casement and the white of a curtain, and the film made of it something almost holy.

In Havana I made a small project of strangers — fourteen of them, one frame each, a roll and a half over two weeks. I shot them on Tri-X because a city that old and that proud deserves the gravity of black and white. Number fourteen looked straight down the lens of my old M4 and did not blink, and I have wondered ever since what he was thinking.

Colour as a kind of weather

The wall in Marrakesh was the red of a slow sunset and I waited an hour for someone to stand against it. Portra took that red and made it sing without ever shouting — the genius of the film is its restraint, the way it pulls a scene toward warmth without ever tipping into nostalgia.

And colour as joy

Then there are the frames you don't plan at all. A laugh in Mexico City, caught between one breath and the next; the gold of a California evening with friends who didn't know I was shooting. These are the photographs I'd run back into a burning house for.

The light always goes

In Santorini I watched two strangers become a silhouette as the sun dropped into the Aegean, and I made one frame, the last on the roll. Goodnight, I thought, to them and to the light and to the day. There was no second chance and I did not want one.

Chapter Five

Coming Home to the Light

Paris · Havana · Copenhagen · 2020 — 2023

In the end I stopped chasing and started staying. The cafés of Paris in the grey hush of a closed-down spring. A Sunday car the colour of a boiled sweet, idling on a Havana street. A window in Copenhagen with the lamp left on, the most domestic and tender light there is. The world had narrowed, and so had I, and I found there was a whole life inside the smaller frame.

Kodak Gold is a humble film, the stuff of family albums and disposable cameras, and I have grown to love it for exactly that. It does not flatter. It simply remembers, the way your grandmother's photographs remember — a little soft, a little gold, entirely true to the feeling if not the fact.

The last frame in this essay is the most ordinary one I have ever made: a room, a window, an afternoon. After twenty-eight years and a hundred thousand miles, I came home to find that the light I had been chasing across the whole turning world had been falling, all along, through the window of an ordinary room, asking only that I stop long enough to see it.

I shot it on the same Leica M6 I have carried since 1998, on the same Portra 400 I have always loved, and when I held the developed frame up to my own window I understood that this was the photograph the entire life had been practising for. Not the grandest. Just the truest. The light comes in. You keep what you can. The rest, you let go.

This essay gathers thirty frames made between 1995 and 2023, on twelve cameras and a dozen emulsions. Everything here was shot on film, developed in chemistry, and scanned with as light a hand as patience allowed. Nothing was generated; everything was found.
