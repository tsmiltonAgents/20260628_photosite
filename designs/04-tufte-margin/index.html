Marginalia · No. IV

Notes on Light & the Slow Frame

A scholarly essay on the discipline of film photography — what the negative remembers, and what the margins have to say about it.

Essay & photographs by Tom Milton · 1996–2012

iOn Light

I have spent thirty years learning to wait for light rather than to chase it. The chase belongs to a younger man, one who believed a photograph could be taken; the waiting belongs to whoever I have since become, who suspects a photograph can only be received. Light is not a resource you spend. It is a guest who arrives on its own schedule, lingers exactly as long as it pleases, and is gone before you have decided what to make of it.1

The first time this truth arrived with any force was on a hillside in Tuscany, late in a September that had already begun to turn the wheat to brass. I had set up the tripod an hour early — habit, superstition, patience masquerading as both — and for fifty-eight of those sixty minutes there was nothing to photograph but a flat, ordinary field. Then the sun dropped low enough to rake across the stubble, every stalk threw a shadow as long as a sentence, and the whole hill ignited. I made one frame. The sunburst, when I had it printed months later, looked less like a record of the field than like a memory of having stood there.

That is the strange economy of the medium. Digital sensors are generous; they will give you a hundred frames of the same indifferent field and let you sort the question out at home. Film is miserly, and its meanness is a kind of instruction. With thirty-six exposures and no screen on the back to reassure you, the camera stops being a net you cast and becomes a question you must answer before you press the shutter: is this the light, or only some light?2

iiThe Slow Frame

There is a fog that settles in the Cascades on certain autumn mornings, so total that the mountains seem to have agreed, overnight, to be rumours of themselves.2 I drove four hours for that fog and was rewarded with the privilege of standing in it, half-blind, for the better part of a dawn. The Pentax 67 is a heavy, deliberate machine — a camera that does not let you forget you are carrying it — and there is a wisdom encoded in its weight. You do not raise such a thing casually. You raise it when you mean it.

I think often about the difference between speed and haste. A fast shutter is not the same as a hurried mind. The slow frame I am defending is not slow on the negative — some of my favourite exposures are a thousandth of a second — but slow in the approach to it: the long arithmetic of standing still, reading the meter, doubting the meter, trusting the eye.3 In the forest the Hasselblad taught me this most plainly. Among the old growth of the Olympic peninsula the light comes down in shafts, as through the clerestory of a cathedral, and it will not be rushed into a frame any more than the trees themselves will.

The coast taught the opposite lesson. At Big Sur the light is never still; it is weather as much as illumination, scudding and reforming, and the only patience that serves you there is the patience to keep the camera ready and accept that the photograph will choose its own instant.4 The Contax in my hands felt almost weightless after the Pentax, and I made the frame at the edge of the cliff in a single breath, the way you catch a falling cup — not by deciding to, but by already having decided.

The negative does not remember what you intended. It remembers only what was true of the light in the instant the shutter was open. That honesty is the whole of the discipline.

iiiSilence & Water

Not all of my negatives are warm. There are days the world withholds its colour, and the only honest answer is black-and-white film, which is not the absence of colour but the presence of structure — light reduced to its grammar of tone. In a stand of pines in Norway, in a silence so complete it had texture, I loaded a roll of HP5 and exposed for the shadows, knowing the fog would take care of the rest.5 Some photographs are about what is there. That one is about how much can be left out.

Water has been my most patient teacher of exposure. In Iceland the falls run all day and ask nothing of you, which means the photograph is entirely your responsibility — there is no decisive moment to excuse a lazy frame. I metered for the spray and let the slow film draw the white of the water into a single continuous gesture.6 Velvia is an unforgiving stock, saturated to the edge of honesty, and it rewards only the photographer who has already made every other decision correctly.

And then there is the hour before all of this, the dawn, when the lake at Banff lay so still that the cabin was published twice, once in timber and once in water.7 Ektar holds a blue like no other emulsion I know; it gave the cold of that morning a body. I have come to believe that the calmest photographs are the most demanding, because there is nothing dramatic to hide behind. Stillness audits you.

ivPortraits of Strangers

To photograph a landscape you need only patience. To photograph a stranger you need a kind of nerve that is closer to courtesy than to courage — the willingness to ask, with the camera, for a moment of someone's life, and the grace to accept it if they decline. In Havana I made a habit of it: a small project I kept, with no great ambition, called simply Strangers, each frame numbered as I made it. Number fourteen was a man on a doorstep who looked at the Leica for exactly as long as it took, and not one second more.8

The window is the portraitist's oldest collaborator, and the most reliable. In Lisbon a woman sat in the soft fall of north light from a tall casement, and the Pentax — that same deliberate weight I carry into forests — slowed the encounter to the pace of a conversation.9 Portra renders skin the way memory does: a little warmer than the truth, forgiving in the highlights, kind. I have never wanted my portraits to be accurate. I have wanted them to be fair.

If there is a thread that runs through all of it — the field, the fog, the falls, the faces — it is this: the photograph is not the thing I saw. It is the residue of my attention to the thing I saw. The road in Vermont that October was beautiful before I arrived and remained beautiful after I left; what the negative holds is the particular quality of my looking, on that afternoon, with that film, in that light that I had finally, after all these years, simply waited for.10

I leave the margins to do what margins do best: to hold the facts — the camera, the film, the place, the year, the small arithmetic of aperture and time — so that the body of the page is free to hold something harder to measure. Read the notes if you are curious how a photograph was made. Read the photographs if you want to know why.
