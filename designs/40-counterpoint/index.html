Counter­point.

Twenty-eight years of film, gathered as an essay. The reading runs in one column; the camera keeps its own notes in the other — emulsion, body, the hour the shutter fell.

Words & pictures — Tom MiltonSet in Fraunces & Spectral
Chapter ILight

On the discipline
of waiting

A landscape is mostly patience. The frame is decided long before the light arrives to fill it.

There is a particular hour in wheat country when the field stops being green and becomes, for perhaps four minutes, the colour of a struck match. I had been standing in the same furrow since mid-afternoon, the Leica cold against my sternum, doing the only work a landscape photographer truly does, which is nothing — and doing it precisely. The exposure was settled. The composition was settled. What remained was to wait for the sun to lower itself the last few degrees and set the whole hillside alight.

Portra forgives a great deal, but it does not forgive impatience. The film holds a highlight the way a good listener holds a pause, and so I let the meter sit a third of a stop hot and trusted the negative to draw the rest back. When the light came it came all at once, the way it always does, and I made four frames before it was gone.

Frame 01 Last Light, Wheat Country
Tuscany, Italy — 1998
Leica M6 · 50mm
Kodak Portra 400
f/8 · 1/250s · +0.3 EV
18:42, low sun

In the old growth of the Olympic peninsula the light works differently — it does not arrive, it filters, descending through two hundred feet of canopy until it reaches the floor already softened and impossibly green. You do not wait for that light. You wait for the wind to stop, so that the long exposure can hold the ferns still. The Hasselblad makes you slow regardless; the waist-level finder turns the forest upside down and asks you to compose a world inverted, which is, I have come to think, the correct relationship between a photographer and a forest.

Frame 02 Cathedral of Pines
Olympic NP, USA — 1996
Hasselblad 500CM · 80mm
Kodak Portra 160
f/16 · 1/30s · −0.7 EV
11:10, overcast

Chapter IIWater

Everything that
reflects

Water is the most patient teacher of exposure. It asks nothing of you, which means the photograph is entirely your responsibility.

At Hallstatt the lake holds the village twice — once standing and once dissolved, the second a little softer than the first, as memory tends to be. I set the RZ67 on the tripod before dawn and metered for the reflection rather than the houses, because the reflection is where the photograph actually lives. Ektar is a saturated, literal film, and it renders that doubled world without sentiment; the sentiment, if there is to be any, has to be yours.

I have made a quiet career out of standing at the edges of cold water before the rest of the world is awake. Big Sur, the Lake District, the falls in Iceland that run all day and ask nothing of anyone — each one taught the same lesson, which is that the surface is never the subject. The subject is what the surface agrees, for a moment, to show you.

Frame 03 Reflections, Still Morning
Hallstatt, Austria — 2000
Mamiya RZ67 · 110mm
Kodak Ektar 100
f/11 · 1/60s · +0.3 EV
06:05, dead calm

The falls in Iceland were the opposite problem. Where the lake demanded stillness, the water there demanded a decision about time itself. A quarter of a second draws the spray into one continuous white gesture; a thousandth freezes every separate drop into something brittle and untrue. Velvia, unforgiving and saturated to the very edge of honesty, rewards only the photographer who has already made every other choice correctly. I metered for the spray, let the slow film do the rest, and walked back across the bridge wet to the knees.

Iceland — 2006

Chapter IIIPeople

A little warmer
than the truth

I have never wanted my portraits to be accurate. I have wanted them to be fair.

The window is the portraitist's oldest collaborator and the most reliable. In Lisbon a woman sat in the soft fall of north light from a tall casement, and the Pentax — that same deliberate weight I carry into forests — slowed the encounter to the pace of a conversation. There is no hurrying a 67; you load ten frames and you spend them like coins. Portra renders skin the way memory does, a little warmer than the truth, forgiving in the highlights, kind.

Not every portrait is made in kindness, and not every face wants to be made warm. In Havana an older man held my eye for the length of a single frame of Tri-X — no softening, no second chance, the grain rising in the shadow of his cheek like weather. Monochrome tells the truth the colour film politely declines to. I left that negative exactly as it was exposed.

Frame 05 By the Window
Lisbon, Portugal — 2011
Pentax 67 · 105mm
Kodak Portra 400
f/2.8 · 1/125s
north light, soft Frame 06 Stranger, No. 14
Havana, Cuba — 2012
Leica M4 · Kodak Tri-X 400

The freckled light of a studio afternoon is its own small kindness. With nothing to wait for and nothing to chase, a portrait becomes a matter of attention only — the way the catchlight sits, the half-second before a real expression replaces the arranged one. That half-second is the entire art. Everything before it is preparation, and everything after it is editing.
